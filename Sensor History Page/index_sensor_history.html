Temperature

Monitoring temperature in hydroponics systems is crucial for maintaining optimal growing conditions and ensuring the health and productivity of plants. Temperature directly impacts plant metabolism, nutrient absorption, and overall growth. Fluctuations in temperature can lead to stress, reduced nutrient uptake, and susceptibility to diseases and pests. By regularly monitoring temperature levels, growers can make informed adjustments to maintain the ideal temperature range, promote healthy plant development, optimize nutrient absorption, and prevent potential issues such as nutrient deficiencies, root rot, or fungal infections. Consistent temperature monitoring is therefore essential for maximizing crop yields, improving resource efficiency, and ensuring the success of hydroponic cultivation.

Stress and Reduced Growth: Fluctuations in temperature can stress plants, leading to reduced growth rates and overall productivity. Without monitoring, plants may struggle to maintain optimal metabolic processes, impacting their ability to absorb nutrients and develop properly.
 Nutrient Imbalances: Temperature fluctuations can affect the solubility and availability of nutrients in the water solution. Without monitoring, nutrient imbalances may occur, leading to deficiencies or toxicities that can harm plant health and compromise crop quality.
 Increased Risk of Diseases and Pests: Temperature variations can create favorable conditions for the growth of pathogens and pests. Without proper monitoring and control, the risk of diseases such as root rot or infestations by insects and fungi increases, potentially causing significant damage to crops and reducing yields.

Nutrients play a vital role in hydroponics systems as they serve as the primary source of essential elements for plant growth and development. In a hydroponic setup, nutrients are directly dissolved in water and delivered to plant roots, eliminating the need for soil. This method allows for precise control over nutrient levels   and ensures that plants receive the right balance of nutrients they need for optimal growth.

The system will read the nutrient levels and decide when more nutrients should be injected into the system. The system will automatically inject the needed nutrients.

Failure to monitor nutrient levels can have numerous consequences:

Nutrient deficiencies : Without proper monitoring, plants may experience deficiencies in essential nutrients such as nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium, or micronutrients like iron, magnesium, and calcium, leading to stunted growth and poor health.
 Nutrient imbalances : Inaccurate nutrient levels can result in imbalances, causing nutrient lockout or toxicity, which can damage plant roots and foliage. (this is especially important in closed systems such hydroponics systems, since the only way to get rid of the nutrients is through plant usage)
 Reduced yield and quality : Insufficient or excessive nutrients can impact plant yield and quality, affecting crop productivity and plant health.
 Pest and disease susceptibility : Plants with nutrient deficiencies or imbalances are more susceptible to pest infestations and diseases, weakening their overall resilience and health.

Just like water level monitoring, flow level monitoring is essential in a hydroponics system to ensure the proper circulation and distribution of nutrient solutions to plants . By monitoring the flow level, the teachers can help maintain consistent nutrient delivery, prevent clogs or blockages in the system and optimize water and nutrient usage.

Just as the water level monitoring, the system will notify the user if the flow is either steadily decreasing or if the flow level is at a dangerous level.

The consequences to for not monitoring flow levels are as follows:

Uneven nutrient distribution : Inconsistent flow levels may result in uneven distribution of nutrients to plants, leading to nutrient deficiencies or excesses in certain areas.
 Clogging or blockages : Without monitoring, clogs or blockages in the system can occur, hindering nutrient flow and potentially damaging plants.
 Root suffocation : Insufficient flow levels can cause roots to become waterlogged or suffocated, leading to root rot and poor plant health.
 Delayed maintenance : Insufficient flow levels may be due to system component damage such as pump or pipe damage, not monitoring flow levels may delay component maintenance (this is especially important since South Africa is subject to Load Shedding, that is known to cause damage to electronic devices).
